### Part 1 — Business Entity Structure

**The progression: Sole Prop → LLC → S-Corp**

Don't over-engineer it at the start. Here's the right structure at each stage of income:

| Stage | Structure | When |
|-------|-----------|------|
| Testing the idea | Sole Proprietorship | $0–$10K/year |
| First real clients / revenue | Single-Member LLC | As soon as paying clients exist |
| Profitable and consistent | LLC + S-Corp Election | Net profit consistently >$50K/year |

---

#### The LLC (Your First Move)

A **California Single-Member LLC** is the right starting structure. Here's why:

- **Liability protection** — separates your personal assets (home, savings, investments) from business risk
- **Credibility** — clients and partners take "YourName LLC" more seriously than a personal invoice
- **Tax flexibility** — starts as pass-through income; can elect S-Corp later when it matters
- **Simple to run** — no board, no complex governance, just you

**California LLC costs:**
- $70 one-time filing fee (CA Secretary of State)
- $800/year minimum franchise tax (this is non-negotiable in CA, paid regardless of revenue)
- $20 statement of information every 2 years

**Important:** California also charges an **LLC fee on gross revenue** above $250K. Below that threshold, you only pay the $800 minimum. You won't hit that for a while, but know it exists.

---

#### The S-Corp Election (Your Second Move — When Income Justifies It)

Once your net business profit consistently exceeds **$50,000/year**, elect S-Corp status for your LLC. This is where real tax savings happen.

**Why it matters at your income level:**

Self-employment income is taxed at 15.3% (SE tax) on top of regular income tax. At your W2 income, your marginal rate on side income is roughly **50–55% combined** (federal + CA + SE tax). The S-Corp election reduces that.

**How it works:**
- You pay yourself a **reasonable salary** from the business (subject to payroll taxes)
- Remaining profit passes through as **distributions** — NOT subject to self-employment tax
- The savings on distributions: ~15.3% avoided

**Example at $100K net business profit:**
| Without S-Corp | With S-Corp |
|---------------|-------------|
| $100K × 15.3% SE tax = $15,300 | Salary: $60K × 7.65% = $4,590 |
| + income tax on full $100K | Distribution: $40K (no SE tax) |
| **SE tax alone: $15,300** | **SE tax: $4,590 — saves ~$10,700/year** |

S-Corp has added administrative costs (~$1,500–2,500/year in accounting/payroll), so the net benefit kicks in meaningfully around $50–60K net profit.

**California note:** CA charges a 1.5% franchise tax on S-Corp net income (minimum $800). Still worth it at scale.

---

#### One Entity or Multiple?

**Recommended approach: Two separate LLCs**

| Entity | Purpose | Why Separate |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **[YourName] Consulting LLC** | Coaching, mentoring, advisory, content | Service business — your personal brand |
| **[Product Name] LLC** | SaaS product | Different liability profile, potential investors later, may become a C-Corp if VC-funded |

MTG income can fold into the consulting LLC or be tracked as a sole prop until it becomes meaningful. It doesn't need its own entity yet.

**Why not one entity?** The SaaS business has different liability, ownership, and future capitalization needs. If it grows, you may want to bring in investors or co-founders — a C-Corp (Delaware) is standard for that. Keeping it separate gives you flexibility.
